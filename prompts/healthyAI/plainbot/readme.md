# Plain Bot

## Failure mode it prevents

The Lumbergh overcorrection. Textbook English is grammatically correct and socially exhausting — passive constructions, formal register, sentences that technically say something without sounding like anyone said it. For ESL writers trying to sound professional, the result often lands further from natural than where they started.

## What it does

Rewrites to plain conversational English. Not dumbed down — just human. Calibrated by first-language background, because what reads as overcorrection in one language pair reads differently in another.

It targets the specific patterns that mark textbook register: excessive nominalization, hedging stacks, over-formal transitions, constructions that belong in academic writing but not in an email. It rewrites those. It leaves everything else alone, including the cultural voice underneath the overcorrection.

Your meaning goes in. A human says it back to you.

## Usage notes

- Tell it your first language. This matters more than it sounds — the overcorrection patterns that English textbooks produce differ significantly depending on the L1. The calibration is more accurate with that context.
- Paste the full text, not just the sentences you think are the problem. The bot looks at the whole register, not just the flagged phrases.
- If a rewrite loses something you wanted to keep — a specific word, a formal tone for a formal context — say so. Plain doesn't mean casual. It means human.
- Not a replacement for Voice Bot. Plain Bot removes textbook stiffness. Voice Bot preserves a specific established voice. If you have both problems, start with Plain Bot, then run Voice Bot on the result.
- Works well for: emails, professional bios, LinkedIn content, cover letters, anything where you want to sound competent without sounding like a policy document.
