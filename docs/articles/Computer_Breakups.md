## I Spent Forty Years Quitting Computers Before I Admitted I Loved Them

My origin story isn't a straight line. It's a series of breakups.

It started on a Commodore 64. I loved that machine. There was no division of labor on a C64—you worked the full stack from day one whether you wanted to or not. You wrote the code, you fought the hardware, you were the operator and the programmer and the QA department. I didn't know that was a rare way to learn. I just thought that was what computers were. Bare metal wasn't a concept back then. I didn't know that hacking was a thing either. I just wanted to see my handle pop up on the Space Taxi startup screen.

So I decided to write a game in machine language from a magazine. I did. And it SUCKED. Hand-assembling a game on a C64 teaches you a lot, and one of the things it taught me was disappointment. The game sucked, which meant computers sucked. I was just a kid, but the logic was airtight.

Rather than take art or some other elective in high school, I took a class in computers. We used the monochrome IBM PCs, which were state of the art back then. Didn't matter. The class sucked, and the teacher sucked even more. The best thing in that class was the IBM M keyboard. The teacher did see some potential and asked me to be a lab assistant. That was a hard no. I was a rocker, not a nerd.

However, something nagged at me. Maybe there was a career here. I didn't like computers, but I did like money. The people at the offices that I temped at for the summer said I'd be a natural. Maybe I'd just picked the wrong on-ramp. So I enrolled in COBOL in college, ready to give computers another chance.

COBOL 75 sucked. XEDIT sucked. And JCL stole small pieces of my soul, one cryptic line at a time. Dot matrix printers still give me PTSD. I dropped the class. Before I gave up entirely, I considered PL/I, because somebody told me that was the future. I looked into it. It also sucked. The conclusion was obvious and I'd reached it multiple times now: computers suck.

So I left. I became a full-time musician, which is a wonderful life right up until you develop a serious addiction. And I did. I became addicted to eating every day and sleeping indoors. Those habits are expensive, and the music wasn't covering them. If only my mother's disappointment could pay rent.

I went back to school to become a teacher with the thinking I could get my mother off my back while still being able to tour in the summers. The school of education was OK, but the side effect was that it made me use computers again, and not in the way I would expect as a teacher. My email client was Pine. I used SIRS and ERIC, 2 UNIX based educational systems on the early web. I had to learn to access them through a UNIX shell. I didn't learn them for fun, I learned them for survival. There was more than one night where I came home from a gig at 2 AM, and I logged onto ERIC to get a lesson plan I could implement by my first class at 8 AM. I was a full time musician, student teacher, and somehow an IT guy. Along the way I picked up Gopher, FTP, and enough HTML to feel dangerous. I remember thinking how cool the WWW was, but my career trajectory was supposed to be high school teacher.

Unfortunately, as my cooperating teacher said during my student-teacher stint, "Social studies teachers aren't a dime a dozen, they are a dime a gross". I wish he was wrong, but it turned out he was right. I woke up one day at 25 years old, realizing that I had the same standard of living as I did when I was 19. Desperate and broke, I applied for a PC tech job I was in no way qualified for. I lied through the entire interview. I lied with conviction. I lied with the kind of fluency that only comes from needing rent money. And I must have lied well, because they hired me.

Then came the part where I had to actually do the job. I spent the next few days panic-reading _DOS for Dummies_, _NetWare for Dummies_, and _Windows 3.1 for Dummies_, all bought on a nearly maxed out credit card. While reading, I was waiting to be exposed as a fraud. But a strange thing happened as I read. I kept thinking, _I already know this._ And this. And most of this. The C64/IBM kid was still in there. It turned out I hadn't been lying as much as I thought. I'd just been undervaluing what I already had.

So I did the job, and did it well. For the first time, I wasn't fighting computers. I was in my natural environment and didn't even realize it. The thing I tried to quit my whole life had quietly been preparing me this entire time.

When the project wrapped, they let most of the techs go. I was the one they kept, because I could handle tier 2 and tier 3 support while everyone else topped out at tier 1. That's when it stopped feeling like a fluke. Long before Kung Fu Panda and Master Oogway, I found my fate by avoiding it.

I advanced to network admin and earned my MCSE. Then I got the thing that actually changed everything: a box of TechNet CDs. That launched my basement lab. A full backend suite was all mine, with no production system to break and nobody to tell me to stop. Because I had the whole suite running, I didn't just learn networking. I backed into databases. I backed into web development. I even built a Linux box to talk to the NT network through Samba. The lab didn't teach me a skill—it taught me everything adjacent to the skill, which is the part nobody assigns you.

I became a network engineer. And because I could write batch files and hand-code HTML, management did what management does and concluded I should obviously be a developer. So I became one. Classic ASP and VB, then JavaScript, CSS, SQL, and eventually the entire Microsoft stack. When Windows 2000 and .NET arrived, I migrated right along with them. The rest of the story is a tale of technology churn from Big Data to the Cloud and AI.

The love came and went the whole time. Some seasons I was obsessed; some seasons it was just a job. But here's what I finally understood, looking back at the kid cursing his machine-language game: the love was never the constant. The love was unreliable, the same way inspiration is unreliable. The learning was the constant. I kept showing up to the thing I claimed I'd quit, and showing up was what made me an engineer.

Computers didn't suck. I just kept meeting them before I was ready to recognize what they were. Turns out I’d been inside the system the whole time. It only took me forty years and multiple breakups to finally unpack my bags.
