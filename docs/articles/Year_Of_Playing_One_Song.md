## What a Year of Playing One Song a Day Taught Me About Learning Anything

Halfway through 2026, I realized I wasn't celebrating what I'd built. I was celebrating who I'd become while building it.

That realization actually began a year earlier...

**A Song A Day**

In 2025, I made a resolution: record and post one song every day. Although I'll do just about anything if people will clap for me, the views and likes were beside the point. I wanted accountability and a public ledger of my progress. I wanted a finished artifact every single day so that by December I could put January-me next to December-me and actually see whether I'd grown, instead of just feeling like I had.

By the end of the year, I had. I became better as a guitarist and a singer. I noticed. Others noticed. The thing that was the biggest surprise: I became a more disciplined person. As an artist, I had come to rely on inspiration. I found that inspiration is unreliable. It shows up when it feels like it. The growth came from showing up, especially on the days I didn't want to. Some days I had fifteen minutes and nothing else. Just one or two takes, and upload. Improvisation became a friend, and there were days I had to be a musical MacGyver. I recorded Paul Revere on a brush and a storage-bin lid in my in-laws' garage when I was out of town without a guitar. In Germany, Hoochie Coochie Man became a harmonica and vocals take. Every public piano I came across was fair game with the most sentimental one being the one I played at my elementary school graduation over 40 years ago. Of course I played Home Sweet Home by Mötley Crüe.

The constraint didn't get in the way of the practice. It was the practice of making something with whatever you have.

I even came to appreciate the criticism. A thumbs-down meant someone had stopped, listened, and formed an opinion. It's easy to let someone fail quietly. A reaction, even a negative one, means the work was alive enough to provoke a response. They say that failure is an excellent teacher, but it isn't the only one. Daily practice introduces you to an entire classroom of teachers. Boredom teaches discipline. Frustration teaches persistence. Curiosity teaches exploration. Criticism teaches humility. Success expands your sense of what's possible. Inspiration reminds you why you started. The only requirement is that you keep showing up, no matter which teacher is waiting for you or which direction the thumb is pointing.

By the end of the year I wasn't only better at music. I was better at showing up. The daily reps had quietly rewired how I operate. Consistency is one of the most underrated forces available to anyone. It's why the tortoise wins. It's why water can cut through stones. They say you love what you work for, not the other way around. It makes sense to me because I had never loved music as much as I did in 2025.

That set up what came next.

**The Hagwon Year**

In Korea, a hagwon is a private academy — an after-hours school where students go to study, drill, and get better at something with real intensity. The concept stuck with me, so in 2026 I opened a personal Hagwon and enrolled a single student: myself.

I didn't want another vague resolution — the kind that fades by February. I wanted another daily one. The rule was simple: learn or build something every day. The bar was deliberately low. A short tutorial on MARP counted. A snippet of code that ended up getting trashed counted. The point was never the size of the output — it was the rep. I had no excuses like not having a guitar with me. Technology has made learning essentially free and endlessly accessible; the material is all right there, waiting. The only variable left is whether I would engage with it or not.

January was refresher training, planning, and research. February through mid-May were heavy training. May and June were application of learning through building. July is a return to the Hagwon to fill gaps and start a new project.

And like the music, the daily reps compounded. I'm looking at the halfway point and I have:

40+ courses and skill badges across Microsoft, Google, Snowflake, Databricks, and Harvard Online
A YouTube channel
Six white papers published, with 4 more taking shape
Four agentic AI applications, and an ETL streaming pipeline, a mobile application, and an edge device project in the works
A Healthy AI Use repository on GitHub

The output is nice. But it's not the part I'd actually point to. The part that matters is the architecture underneath it. Just like 2025, the habit eventually formed its own gravity. Now a day without learning or building feels off, and I can't call it a night until I've fulfilled something my brain was counting on. My once dormant love for computing has come roaring back to life in 2026. Maybe it's a mid-life crisis, but I feel like a kid again. The youthful enthusiasm is there like before, but it's now honed by earned wisdom.

Over the years I've built what I call mental shelves. These are frameworks within the intellectual warehouse. A new idea doesn't bounce around looking for somewhere to live. It snaps onto a shelf that's been there for years. That's why nothing in 2026 started from zero. Every new idea had a mature ecosystem where the concept could grow and thrive the moment it arrived. Daily practice didn't just give me skills. It gave me velocity — and velocity is what made this halfway point possible in the first place.

**The Honest Question:**

People often tell me they wish they had time to play guitar, learn how to code, or whatever it is they wish they could master. I usually ask the same gentle question back: how much screen time is spent on shows and doomscrolling? Not as a gotcha, but as a genuine reframe. Most of us aren't short on time. We've allocated it somewhere else and called it scarcity. That's a budgeting problem wearing the costume of fate.

Daily practice doesn't ask for hours. It asks for intention. Twenty minutes a day is enough to change the trajectory of a life. It changed mine twice — once through music, once through AI.

So here's the invitation: pick something, anything, and practice it daily for the rest of the year. Not perfectly. Not dramatically. Just daily.

Build your own Hagwon. Build your own shelves. Build your own velocity.

I think you'll be surprised by who's standing there in December.
