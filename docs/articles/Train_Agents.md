## You Don't Configure an Employee. You Train One. Same Goes for Your Agents.

There's a word choice problem at the center of most AI implementations, and it's causing real damage.
Organizations are configuring their AI agents. Adjusting parameters, writing rules, stacking guardrails, tuning settings. That's the mental model — agent as software, deployment as a configuration exercise. Get the settings right and the system behaves correctly.

That's not how inference works.

A configured system follows rules. An inference system makes judgments. And when you're relying on something that makes judgments, the question isn't whether the rules are correct. The question is whether the judgment reflects your organization's values, competitive advantages, and the things that make your operation yours. You can't write a rule for every situation an agent will encounter. That's the whole point of deploying one. What you can do is train it — the same way you train the people who also can't have a rule written for every situation they'll encounter.

When you hire a new employee, you don't hand them a policy manual and call it onboarding. Effective organizational training runs four layers deep: policy and procedure, which tells people what the rules are; culture, which tells them how this organization thinks and operates; values, which tells them what the organization would never do even if no policy explicitly prohibits it; and competitive advantage, which tells them what makes this operation distinctly ours and why that matters in every decision they make. That's not soft content. That's the operating system your employee runs on when the policy manual doesn't cover the situation in front of them.

Your agent needs the same four layers. Not instead of guardrails — in addition to them. Guardrails tell the agent what it cannot do. Training tells the agent what it should want to do. Inference without values is drift. Inference with values is alignment. The gap between those two things is where your brand voice, your judgment calls, your institutional knowledge, and your competitive differentiation either survive the AI transition or get averaged out into generic output.

If your agent sounds like every other company's agent, you didn't train it. You configured it.

Here's where the failure compounds. There are three ways organizations get this wrong, and most are doing all three simultaneously.
The first is prompt engineering as a substitute for training. Teams spend weeks iterating on prompts, patching output quality one sentence at a time, adding clauses and qualifications and examples to the interface layer. Some of that work is legitimate. But if the underlying model has no grounding in your organization's values and context, you are caulking a wall that has no foundation. The prompt is the interface. Training is the foundation. Fixing the interface doesn't fix the foundation.

The second is the rules-only approach — guardrails without values. This produces agents that are technically compliant and operationally useless. They avoid everything prohibited and optimize for nothing in particular. An agent trained only on what it cannot do will make the safest possible choice in every situation, which is frequently not the right choice. Safe and right are not synonyms. Your people know the difference because you trained them on the difference. Your agent needs the same.

The third failure mode is the most subtle and the most expensive: anthropomorphizing the agent during training. Being polite when you should be precise. Being vague when you should be explicit. Softening instructions because bluntness feels unkind. Holding back repetition because it seems redundant. Leaving things implied because a person would understand.

The agent is not a person. It does not have feelings. It will not be demoralized by directness, exhausted by repetition, or offended by clarity. What it will do, reliably, is infer from ambiguity — and if you trained it with ambiguous inputs, it will produce ambiguous outputs and call that judgment.

You can run the same training sequence a thousand times without the agent resenting you for it. You can be stricter than any human environment would tolerate. You can be as explicit as a five-year-old needs you to be. Not because the agent deserves less respect — but because precision is the respect. Vague instructions aren't kind. They're just vague. And vague instructions produce vague agents that your customers and colleagues will eventually stop trusting.

Train your agents on your values. Train them on what good looks like in your specific context. Train them with the bluntness and repetition and clarity that the medium actually supports. And then, when the training is solid, hand them an objective and get out of the way.
